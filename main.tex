% 4. Abstract
\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
Digital avatars could enable more expressive virtual communication, personalized AI assistants, and new forms of telepresence. However, most existing avatar-generation systems focus on producing short video clips. A digital avatar that participates in an ongoing interaction must instead generate video continuously while preserving its identity, responding naturally to speech and facial motion, and remaining visually coherent over long periods of time.

This thesis studies continuous digital avatar generation. Given a target identity and a live driving stream, the system aims to render an animated version of the target that follows the driver's expressions and behavior without inheriting the driver's appearance. We develop a recurrent diffusion-based architecture that maintains visual state across time, allowing the avatar to generate extended sequences without repeatedly processing its entire history.

Our experiments reveal that continuous generation introduces a primary identity-stability failure mode. In the baseline autoregressive system, the avatar gradually loses the target's facial structure and converges toward the identity of the driving person, producing identity leakage in 93\% of evaluated frames under our latent-space diagnostic. We introduce explicit identity anchoring, feature-space projection, and knowledge transfer from a pretrained diffusion transformer to better separate who the avatar is from how it moves. Under teacher-forced evaluation with clean historical context, these methods reduce observed identity leakage to 0\%. However, identity drift can reappear during fully autoregressive generation as small visual errors accumulate over time. We also find that incorporating dense audio representations can disrupt the model's visual memory, highlighting an additional challenge for speech-driven avatars.

These results demonstrate progress toward digital avatars that can sustain a coherent visual presence throughout an interaction. They also identify long-term identity preservation, autoregressive stability, and audiovisual integration as key challenges for building persistent digital humans.

\chapter{Introduction}

Digital avatars could make remote communication feel less like exchanging compressed video streams and more like sharing a persistent social presence. Prior face reenactment and neural talking-head systems show that a target face can be animated from a source driver~\cite{thies2016face, wang2021facevid2vid, drobyshev2022megaportraits}. More broadly, recent interactive generative simulators such as NeuralOS show the relevance of maintaining recurrent visual state while rendering frames in response to live inputs~\cite{rivard2026neuralos}. A useful persistent avatar, however, should not merely generate a short clip of a face. It must remain available throughout an interaction, preserve the appearance of a target person, and respond continuously to another person's motion, expression, and speech. This thesis studies that long-horizon version of avatar generation: given a target identity and a live driving stream, generate an animated target avatar that follows the driver without becoming the driver.

\section{Persistent Digital Avatars as a Long-Horizon Generation Problem}

The central difficulty is temporal persistence. In a short video, a model can often hide small geometric mistakes because the generation ends before those mistakes accumulate. In a live interaction, every generated frame becomes part of the history that conditions the next frame. Small errors in facial structure, pose, or texture therefore become self-conditioning signals. Over a long autoregressive rollout, these signals can grow into visible drift.

For digital avatars, this drift is especially damaging because the model must preserve a stable answer to a simple question: \emph{who is on screen?} The avatar should borrow the source user's expression, head motion, and mouth dynamics, but not the source user's bone structure, skin tone, or facial proportions. A continuous avatar model therefore has to separate identity from motion while operating under autoregressive feedback.

\section{The Central Tension: Who Versus How}

We formulate the avatar task as a bipartite conditioning problem. The \emph{target avatar} supplies identity: the stable geometry and appearance that should persist across the interaction. The \emph{source user} supplies motion: pose, expression, and other dynamic behavior. The model must combine these two streams without allowing the static identity information in the source stream to dominate the target identity.

Our experiments show that this separation is not automatic. In the baseline system, the generated avatar gradually moves toward the driver's facial structure. We refer to this failure mode as \textit{source identity bleed}: the recurrent visual state finds a strong static attractor in the driver stream and begins to copy it. Under autoregressive cross-identity evaluation, this baseline produced identity leakage in 93\% of evaluated frames, making identity preservation the central empirical problem of the thesis.

\section{Why Existing Video Diffusion Backbones Struggle}

Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs)~\cite{rombach2022highresolution} and Diffusion Transformers (DiTs)~\cite{peebles2023scalable} provide strong image and video generation backbones, building on denoising diffusion models~\cite{ho2020denoising} and Transformer self-attention~\cite{vaswani2017attention}. Continuous avatars stress them in two ways. First, diffusion video generation is vulnerable to exposure bias: during training, the model sees clean historical context, while during inference it must condition on its own imperfect generations~\cite{bengio2015scheduled}. Second, maintaining a long visual history with standard self-attention is expensive because attention scales quadratically, $\mathcal{O}(L^2)$, in the sequence length $L$.

Structured State Space Models (SSMs)~\cite{gu2022efficiently}, and Mamba-2 in particular~\cite{dao2024transformers}, offer a way to process long histories with linear complexity by compressing past tokens into a recurrent hidden state. This makes them attractive for continuous generation. However, their 1D causal scan also introduces a visual inductive bias: spatial tokens from a 2D face must be flattened into a sequence, and identity information must survive finite recurrent memory~\cite{zhu2024vision, phung2024dimsum}. Thus, linear scaling is not the thesis goal by itself. It is the architectural constraint under which the harder avatar-stability problem must be solved.

\section{Approach and Findings}

This thesis develops a recurrent diffusion backbone that combines a DiT-style denoising scaffold with a Mamba-2 Structured State Space Duality (SSD) sequence mixer~\cite{dao2024transformers}. The model processes a dual-stream context window containing source-user motion, target-avatar history, a clean identity anchor, and the noisy target frame to be denoised. To make this structure work for persistent avatars, we introduce identity anchoring, visual scan corrections, state-space decoupling, latent-space projection, and feature distillation from a pretrained diffusion transformer~\cite{t2md2025}.

The resulting system demonstrates a clear boundary. Under teacher-forced evaluation, where the historical context is clean, the proposed interventions reduce observed identity leakage to 0\% on the evaluated cross-identity pairs. This shows that the model can separate who the avatar is from how it moves when clean context is available. Under fully autoregressive generation, however, identity drift can reappear as small visual errors accumulate, indicating that temporal self-conditioning remains the dominant remaining limitation. We also find that dense EnCodec audio tokens~\cite{defossez2023high} can destabilize the visual state, exposing audio-visual alignment as an important future challenge.

\section{Core Contributions}

This thesis makes four contributions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Problem formulation and diagnostics for continuous avatar identity stability.} We formalize persistent avatar generation as a dual-stream identity-motion problem and introduce latent-space diagnostics for measuring source identity bleed, including the user-leakage rate and mean swap score.
    
    \item \textbf{A Mamba-2 DiT hybrid architecture for recurrent avatar generation.} We replace the quadratic self-attention mixer in a DiT-style diffusion backbone with a linear Mamba-2 SSD sequence mixer, while preserving diffusion timestep conditioning through AdaLN and MLP pathways.
    
    \item \textbf{Visual stabilization methods for separating identity from motion.} We introduce the Anchor-Last context layout, Sweep-4 spatial scanning, state-space decoupling, Rank-1 Null-Space Projection, and Transformer-to-Mamba Feature Distillation (T2MD)~\cite{t2md2025}. Together, these interventions reduce observed identity leakage to 0\% under teacher-forced evaluation on the evaluated cross-identity pairs.
    
    \item \textbf{A documented analysis of audio-visual integration failures.} We show that high-bandwidth 32-codebook EnCodec conditioning can flush the finite SSM visual memory, smear audio information across non-mouth regions, and desynchronize under naive 60 fps audio to 15 fps video indexing. These findings define the next stage of work for speech-driven continuous avatars.
\end{itemize}

\chapter{Background \& Related Work}

Continuous digital avatars sit at the intersection of face reenactment~\cite{thies2016face}, image animation~\cite{siarohin2019first}, neural talking-head synthesis~\cite{wang2021facevid2vid}, video diffusion, recurrent sequence modeling, and audio-visual generation. Adjacent work on recurrent diffusion-based interface simulation, such as NeuralOS, reinforces the broader importance of maintaining persistent visual state under interactive control~\cite{rivard2026neuralos}. This chapter reviews these areas in the order needed by the thesis argument. We first situate the avatar task and dataset setting, then review latent diffusion and Diffusion Transformers, then introduce Structured State Space Models (SSMs) and Mamba-2 as a long-context alternative. The final sections focus on the failure modes that become central in later chapters: visual scan bias, conditioning collapse, exposure bias, and high-variance audio conditioning.

\section{Digital Avatars and Continuous Face Generation}

Digital avatar generation differs from generic video generation because its degrees of freedom are semantically constrained. The model is not free to hallucinate a new person at each time step. It must preserve a target identity while importing motion from a separate driving signal, a goal shared by image-animation and neural-head-avatar systems~\cite{siarohin2019first, drobyshev2022megaportraits}. This creates a dual-stream structure: one stream defines \emph{who} should appear, and the other defines \emph{how} that person should move.

Most successful face-generation systems are evaluated over short clips or independently generated windows~\cite{siarohin2019first, wang2021facevid2vid, drobyshev2022megaportraits}. Persistent interaction is stricter. Once an avatar is deployed in an ongoing conversation, the model repeatedly conditions on its own past outputs. Identity preservation therefore becomes a long-horizon state-estimation problem rather than a single-frame image synthesis problem. The core challenge is not only visual fidelity, but stable disentanglement between static identity and dynamic motion.

\section{Dyadic Interaction Datasets}

Training an online avatar requires data in which two participants interact over time, not just single-person talking-head clips. The CANDOR corpus provides a large collection of naturalistic video-chat conversations between strangers, with audio, video, transcripts, and survey metadata~\cite{reece2023candor}. It is well suited for studying conversational behavior, but its video-chat format and broad social-science annotations are not optimized for the paired in-person avatar generation pipeline used in this thesis.

We therefore use the Seamless Interaction dataset as the primary data source~\cite{seamlessinteraction2025}. Seamless contains over 4{,}000 hours of face-to-face dyadic interaction footage from more than 4{,}000 participants, with synchronized video, audio, transcripts, facial/body keypoints, voice activity detection, and interaction metadata. Its two-participant structure is directly aligned with the thesis task: one stream can serve as the live source user, while the other provides target-avatar history and identity context. Because the full dataset is very large, this thesis uses cropped, downsampled face-video subsets as a proof of concept.

\section{Latent Diffusion and Autoregressive Video Generation}

Latent Diffusion Models have become a standard backbone for high-quality generative computer vision. By compressing high-dimensional pixel space into a lower-dimensional latent manifold via a Variational Autoencoder (VAE)~\cite{kingma2014autoencoding}, LDMs can perform the iterative denoising process of diffusion models~\cite{ho2020denoising} with significantly higher computational efficiency~\cite{rombach2022highresolution}.

Extending LDMs from images to video introduces temporal dependencies~\cite{blattmann2023align}. A standard autoregressive video generator uses a sliding context window in which previously generated frames condition future frames. This design is natural for continuous avatars, but it exposes the model to \textit{exposure bias}: during training the historical context is clean, while during inference the history includes the model's own imperfect generations~\cite{bengio2015scheduled}. Because visual latents are continuous rather than discrete, small out-of-distribution errors can accumulate into structural drift over long rollouts~\cite{statespacediffuser2025}.

\section{Diffusion Transformers and the Attention Bottleneck}

Historically, LDMs relied on U-Net backbones with spatial convolutions and downsampling~\cite{rombach2022highresolution}. Diffusion Transformers (DiTs) replace that convolutional backbone with a transformer-style architecture~\cite{vaswani2017attention} that patchifies latent representations and processes them through multi-head self-attention, Adaptive Layer Normalization (AdaLN), and MLP blocks~\cite{peebles2023scalable}. This design provides expressive latent routing and has become a strong baseline for scalable diffusion models.

The limitation for continuous avatars is context length. Self-attention scales quadratically, $\mathcal{O}(L^2)$, with sequence length $L$~\cite{peebles2023scalable}. A persistent avatar model must maintain source-user motion, target-avatar history, identity anchors, and the current denoising target across time. As the context grows, the attention matrix becomes the computational bottleneck. This motivates replacing the attention mixer with a recurrent sequence mixer that can preserve long history without reprocessing every pairwise token interaction.

\section{Structured State Space Models and Mamba-2}

Structured State Space Models provide a recurrent alternative to attention for long sequences~\cite{gu2022efficiently}. Mamba-2 extends this family through Structured State Space Duality (SSD), which connects a recurrent view for inference with a chunked matrix-computation view for training~\cite{dao2024transformers}. In its recurrent form, the state update can be written as:
$$h_t = \bar{A}h_{t-1} + \bar{B}x_t.$$
By compressing historical context into a fixed-size hidden state, Mamba-2 enables sequence processing with linear time complexity in $L$ and constant recurrent memory per step.

This property makes Mamba-2 attractive for continuous avatars. At inference time, the model can hand off the discrete convolutional and SSM states between frames, avoiding full recomputation of the entire history. On modern GPUs, the fused SSD and \texttt{causal\_conv1d} kernels also align well with BF16 execution, making the approach computationally plausible on H100-class hardware.

\section{Visual SSM Inductive Biases}

The same recurrence that makes Mamba-2 efficient also creates a visual modeling problem. Mamba is fundamentally a 1D causal sequence model. Face latents, however, are spatial tensors. Flattening a 2D face into a 1D token stream disrupts spatial locality, introduces scan-direction bias, and asks finite recurrent memory to carry high-frequency geometry across the sequence.

Prior visual SSM work identifies this issue as a linear-scan pitfall~\cite{zhu2024vision, phung2024dimsum}. Without correction, a pure sequential scan can favor low-frequency structure over localized detail and can destabilize diffusion denoising. For avatar generation, the consequence is especially severe: if identity geometry is compressed or forgotten, the model may regress toward the strongest static signal in the context, which is often the driving user's face.

\section{Identity Preservation, Conditioning Collapse, and Exposure Bias}

The dual-stream avatar setting exposes a specific form of conditioning collapse. The model must use source-user latents for motion without absorbing the source user's identity. When training uses deterministic teacher forcing, the recurrent state may never learn to recover from the noisy histories it will see during deployment~\cite{t2md2025, statespacediffuser2025}. During autoregressive inference, minor latent errors then compound and can push the hidden state toward an unintended identity attractor.

Several strategies are relevant to this failure mode. Scheduled sampling and context-noise injection expose the model to corrupted histories during training~\cite{bengio2015scheduled}. Feature distillation can transfer stable representations from attention-based teachers to recurrent students~\cite{phung2024dimsum, t2md2025}. In hybrid diffusion-SSM architectures, state-space decoupling is also important: if diffusion gradients freely rewrite the recurrent memory, the sequence mixer can lose the temporal inductive bias that made it useful.

\section{Audio-Visual Conditioning for Speech-Driven Avatars}

A complete conversational avatar must eventually respond to speech as well as facial motion. Audio conditioning is attractive because it can drive mouth shape, timing, and subtle speaking dynamics, as demonstrated by speech-to-lip generation systems such as Wav2Lip~\cite{prajwal2020wav2lip}. However, dense audio representations are not naturally aligned with spatial visual tokens. High-bandwidth EnCodec streams~\cite{defossez2023high}, for example, provide many discrete codebook tokens per visual frame. If these tokens are inserted into a finite-capacity recurrent visual state without structure, they can behave as high-variance noise.

This thesis therefore treats audio as both a goal and a boundary. The visual experiments establish how to stabilize identity under motion-driven generation. The audio chapter later documents why naive high-bandwidth audio integration disrupts that stability, and why future systems need localized spatial routing and stride-aware temporal indexing.

\chapter{Task Formulation, Dataset, and Diagnostics}\label{ch:task}

This chapter defines the experimental problem studied in the thesis. Before introducing the architecture, we specify what the model receives, what it must generate, how success is measured, and why the baseline failure is severe enough to motivate the stabilization methods that follow.

\section{Task Definition}

The goal is one-step continuous avatar generation in latent space. At each time step, the model receives a short history of source-user frames, a short history of target-avatar frames, an identity anchor for the target avatar, and a noisy latent for the target frame to be denoised. It must predict the denoising residual for the next avatar frame:
\[
    \epsilon_\theta =
    f_\theta(\mathbf{U}_{1:T}, \mathbf{V}_{1:T}, \mathbf{A}, \mathbf{Z}_t, t),
\]
where $\mathbf{U}_{1:T}$ denotes source-user visual context, $\mathbf{V}_{1:T}$ denotes target-avatar history, $\mathbf{A}$ denotes the clean target identity anchor, $\mathbf{Z}_t$ denotes the noisy target latent at diffusion timestep $t$, and $\epsilon_\theta$ is the predicted noise.

The source stream should contribute dynamic information such as expression, head pose, and mouth movement. The avatar stream and anchor should contribute stable identity information such as facial proportions, skin tone, and target-specific geometry. The desired behavior is therefore not reconstruction of the source user, but controlled transfer of motion onto the target identity.

\section{Dual-Stream Context and Evaluation Modes}

We evaluate cross-identity pairs in which one user drives a different target avatar. This setting exposes whether the model can separate identity from motion. If the generated latent becomes closer to the source user than to the target avatar, the model has failed the core task even if the frame appears temporally smooth.

Two evaluation modes are used throughout the thesis:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Teacher-forced evaluation} supplies ground-truth historical avatar frames as context. This isolates whether the model can spatially separate target identity from source motion when the past is clean.
    \item \textbf{Autoregressive rollout} feeds generated frames back into the context window. This measures whether the model remains stable when it must condition on its own imperfect outputs.
\end{itemize}

The distinction is essential. Teacher forcing tests identity disentanglement under controlled context, while autoregressive rollout tests long-horizon self-conditioning. A model can succeed in the first setting and still drift in the second.

\section{Dataset and Experimental Setup}

The experiments use the Seamless Interaction dataset~\cite{seamlessinteraction2025}, a large-scale collection of in-person dyadic interactions with synchronized video and audio. This dataset was selected because the thesis task requires two temporally aligned participant streams: a live source stream that supplies motion and a target/avatar stream that supplies identity and history. CANDOR~\cite{reece2023candor} was also considered because it contains naturalistic two-person video-chat conversations, but Seamless is better matched to the face-to-face, paired-stream setting and provides richer embodied-interaction signals for future audio-visual extensions.

The full Seamless release is far larger than the compute budget of this thesis, so the work uses face-centered proof-of-concept subsets. Videos are cropped around the participant's face at $384 \times 384$ resolution, then temporally downsampled from 30 fps to 15 fps. The downsampling doubles the temporal span represented by a fixed context window while keeping GPU memory manageable. For storage efficiency, training reads compressed MP4 videos rather than decompressed image folders. Each MP4 is indexed by a companion CSV with rows of the form \texttt{record\_num,image\_num}, where \texttt{record\_num} identifies the video file and \texttt{image\_num} identifies the frame within that video.

The dataset is used as released for research purposes, and all thesis figures derived from the dataset are presented only as qualitative examples of model behavior. Because this work studies face identity transfer, the quantitative results should be interpreted as proof-of-concept diagnostics on processed subsets rather than as a population-level evaluation of all Seamless participants.

Visual frames are encoded with a KL-regularized latent autoencoder initialized from the public LDM \texttt{kl-f16} checkpoint~\cite{rombach2022highresolution}. The autoencoder compresses face crops by a factor of 16, producing continuous latents of shape $C \times H \times W = 16 \times 16 \times 16$. Before training the recurrent diffusion model, the autoencoder is sanity-checked by overfitting small Seamless subsets and comparing reconstructions against the pretrained checkpoint. Figure~\ref{fig:autoencoder_recon} shows representative reconstructions after this preprocessing stage.

The recurrent diffusion model operates on these latents using a context length of $T=7$ frames per visual stream. The reported cross-identity experiments use source-target pairs drawn from the processed Seamless subsets. We refer to the main development pair as the \emph{primary pair}; the \emph{disparate pair} is a source-target combination with larger visible differences in facial structure and appearance. These terms are used only to distinguish the two evaluated cross-identity conditions in Table~\ref{tab:results_leakage}; they are not intended as a general taxonomy of identity distance. The table reports frame-level latent diagnostics; future work should broaden the evaluation to more identities, longer rollouts, and perceptual identity metrics.

All experiments are conducted on the Narval and Mist HPC clusters using NVIDIA H100 80 GB SXM5 accelerators. Optimization uses AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2019decoupled} with base learning rate $1.5 \times 10^{-4}$, and training is run in \texttt{bf16-mixed} precision to match the Mamba-2 fused kernel execution path. Unless otherwise stated, visual results are reported at 15 fps in latent space after VAE decoding.

For evaluation, we distinguish next-frame teacher forcing from fully autoregressive rollout. In teacher forcing, all previous avatar frames are ground truth. In autoregressive rollout, generated avatar frames are decoded, re-encoded, and fed back into the model as future context. The latter setting is the more realistic test of continuous interaction because prediction errors become part of the model's own input history.

\section{Failure Mode: Source Identity Bleed}

The baseline model reveals a failure mode specific to continuous avatar generation. During autoregressive rollouts, the generated target avatar gradually moves toward the source user's facial structure. The model appears to copy the driver's static geometry because that geometry is a strong and repeatedly observed signal in the context window. We call this failure \textit{source identity bleed}.

This behavior clarifies the thesis problem. The recurrent backbone is not merely forgetting pixels; it is confusing two semantic roles in the context. The source stream should be used for motion, but it also contains identity. The target stream should define identity, but its information must survive finite state-space memory and diffusion noise. The rest of the thesis studies how to make that separation reliable.

\section{Quantitative Diagnostics}

To measure identity bleed, we compute latent-space similarities between the generated target latent, the source user's latent, and the target avatar anchor. Let $\mathbf{z}_t$ be the fully denoised and VAE-decoded-then-re-encoded generated target latent at video frame $t$, $\mathbf{u}_t$ be the source user's driving latent at the same frame, and $\mathbf{a}$ be the clean target avatar anchor. All three tensors are flattened from $16 \times 16 \times 16$ latent tensors into 4096-dimensional vectors before cosine similarity is computed.

The first diagnostic is the user-leakage rate:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Leakage} = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathbb{1} \Big[ \cos(\mathbf{z}_t, \mathbf{u}_t) > \cos(\mathbf{z}_t, \mathbf{a}) \Big].
\end{equation}
This metric reports the fraction of frames whose generated latent is closer to the source user than to the target avatar.

The second diagnostic is the mean swap score:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Swap Score} = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \Big( \cos(\mathbf{z}_t, \mathbf{u}_t) - \cos(\mathbf{z}_t, \mathbf{a}) \Big).
\end{equation}
A positive swap score indicates source-user attraction, while a negative score indicates target-avatar anchoring.

These metrics are diagnostics rather than a complete identity evaluation. VAE latents can entangle identity with expression, pose, illumination, and reconstruction artifacts, so a high source cosine may partly reflect successful motion transfer rather than only identity leakage. We therefore interpret the leakage rate together with visual rollouts and teacher-forced comparisons.

\section{Baseline Result}

The unmitigated autoregressive baseline produced a 93.00\% identity leakage rate and a mean swap score of $+0.342$. This confirms that the model systematically prefers the source user's static geometry over the target avatar's identity during long-horizon rollout. The architectural and training interventions in Chapters~\ref{ch:architecture} and~\ref{ch:visual-results} are designed to reverse this behavior.

\chapter{Model Architecture}\label{ch:architecture}

Chapter~\ref{ch:task} established that continuous avatars need a model that can preserve target identity while importing source motion over long autoregressive horizons. This chapter presents the architecture built around that requirement: a Mamba-2 DiT hybrid that uses linear recurrent sequence mixing for long context, explicit identity anchoring for target preservation, spatial scan corrections for face latents, and decoupled training to protect recurrent memory from diffusion instability.

\section{Design Requirements}\label{sec:design-requirements}

The architecture is guided by five requirements:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Long-horizon memory.} The model must carry visual context across many generated frames without recomputing an ever-growing attention matrix.
    \item \textbf{Identity anchoring.} The target avatar's geometry must remain available immediately before denoising the next frame.
    \item \textbf{Spatial locality.} The 1D recurrent scan must not erase the 2D spatial structure of face latents.
    \item \textbf{Stable diffusion conditioning.} Timestep conditioning must guide denoising without overwriting the recurrent temporal state.
    \item \textbf{Future audio compatibility.} Audio conditioning should be structurally separated from the main visual memory so speech tokens do not flood the SSM state.
\end{itemize}

The sections below introduce the architectural components in this order: the Mamba-2 DiT backbone, the dual-stream token layout, Anchor-Last identity grounding, Sweep-4 spatial scanning, state-space decoupling, computational feasibility, and the audio bypass used as preparation for future multimodal extensions.

\section{The Hybrid Backbone: DiTMambaBlock}\label{sec:hybrid-backbone}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Replacing Self-Attention with Mamba-2 SSD}

In the canonical Diffusion Transformer (DiT) \cite{peebles2023scalable}, each backbone block applies multi-head self-attention to the full token sequence. For autoregressive avatar generation, the context window at inference time comprises $T = 7$ user frames, $T = 7$ avatar history frames, a clean target-avatar identity anchor, and the noisy denoising target. The sequence length is fixed at $L=304$ for the experiments in this thesis, but it would grow with longer context windows or higher-resolution latents. Since self-attention is $\mathcal{O}(L^2)$ in sequence length $L$, it becomes the throughput bottleneck as the context grows.

The Mamba-2 Structured State-Space Duality (SSD) layer \cite{dao2024transformers} provides a linear-time alternative: a selective scan over the input sequence with $\mathcal{O}(L)$ time and $\mathcal{O}(1)$ recurrent state per token. We therefore replace the multi-head self-attention mixer in each of the 12 backbone DiT blocks with a Mamba-2 SSD block (\texttt{model\_mode = 'mamba'} in our implementation), while preserving the Adaptive Layer Normalization with Zero-initialization (AdaLN-Zero) conditioning stack surrounding each block.

Concretely, each \texttt{DiTMambaBlock} computes six AdaLN-Zero modulation parameters~\cite{peebles2023scalable}:

(\texttt{shift\_msa}, \texttt{scale\_msa}, \texttt{gate\_msa}, \texttt{shift\_mlp}, \texttt{scale\_mlp}, \texttt{gate\_mlp}),

\noindent from the diffusion timestep embedding via a zero-initialized SiLU-Linear head. It applies them to the pre-normalized input before and after the Mamba-2 mixer and the subsequent MLP respectively, and adds gated residual connections---identical in structure to the original DiTBlock except that \texttt{nn.MultiheadAttention}~\cite{vaswani2017attention} is replaced by \texttt{mamba\_ssm.Mamba2}.

Because the token sequence interleaves multiple streams (user visual, avatar visual, anchor, and target) each representing a $4 \times 4$ spatial patch grid, a naive 1D causal scan would discard vertical spatial locality within each frame. We therefore wrap the Mamba-2 layer in a Sweep-4 cross-scan: for each frame's contiguous $h \times w$ token block, four directional Mamba passes are run (row-major forward, row-major reverse, column-major forward, column-major reverse) and their outputs are summed before re-insertion into the sequence. This is non-causal only within a single spatial frame; temporal ordering across frames remains causal. The batch-concatenation strategy maximizes kernel utilization by processing all four sweeps in a single Mamba forward call.

The model is implemented with \texttt{hidden\_size = 384}, \texttt{mamba\_expand = 2}, \texttt{mamba\_d\_state = 64}, \texttt{mamba\_d\_conv = 4}, and \texttt{mamba\_headdim = 48}. The \texttt{headdim} choice is constrained by the \texttt{causal\_conv1d} kernel's stride-alignment requirement: the number of groups $\texttt{ngroups} = (\texttt{hidden\_size} \times \texttt{mamba\_expand}) / \texttt{mamba\_headdim}$ must be divisible by 8. With \texttt{mamba\_headdim = 48}, $\texttt{ngroups} = 16$, satisfying this constraint; the default \texttt{headdim = 64} yields $\text{ngroups} = 12$ and is rejected at model initialization. Mamba-2 weights are held in \texttt{bfloat16}, matching the \texttt{bf16-mixed} training precision of the H100, and the implementation relies on the hardware-accelerated fused SSD and \texttt{causal\_conv1d} CUDA kernels provided by the \texttt{mamba\_ssm} package, which map the selective scan computation onto the Tensor Core pipeline.

The resulting token sequence, in the canonical Sweep-4 configuration, follows the order:
\begin{center}
    \texttt{[ user\_1 ... user\_7 | avatar\_1 ... avatar\_7 | anchor x 4 | target ]}
\end{center}
where each visual frame occupies 16 spatial patch tokens (\texttt{patch\_size = 4} over a $16 \times 16$ latent), giving a total sequence length of $L = 7 \times 16 + 7 \times 16 + 4 \times 16 + 1 \times 16 = 304$ tokens. The anchor token is placed last (\texttt{anchor\_position = 'last'}) and repeated four times to compensate for LayerNorm magnitude normalization~\cite{ba2016layer}, ensuring the identity signal remains salient at the end of the causal scan immediately preceding the denoising target.

Audio conditioning (32-codebook EnCodec tokens, 5 tokens per frame) is present in the Sweep-4 model but is not appended to the main Mamba sequence. Instead, it is injected onto the denoising target tokens via a separate, lightweight residual cross-attention bypass (\texttt{audio\_cross\_attn}, zero-gated at initialization), decoupling audio alignment from the linear scan and preserving the $\mathcal{O}(L)$ complexity of the backbone.

\subsection{Layer Anatomy}

Each of the $D=12$ layers in the backbone is an instance of \texttt{DiTMambaBlock},
comprising the following sub-modules in sequential order: %

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{AdaLN-Zero Modulation.} The diffusion timestep embedding
        $\mathbf{c} \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ ($d = 384$) is mapped through a SiLU
        activation and a linear layer to produce six modulation vectors:
        %
        \begin{equation}\label{eq:adaln}
            (\boldsymbol{\gamma}_1, \boldsymbol{\beta}_1, \boldsymbol{\alpha}_1,
             \boldsymbol{\gamma}_2, \boldsymbol{\beta}_2, \boldsymbol{\alpha}_2)
            = \mathrm{Linear}_{6d}\bigl(\mathrm{SiLU}(\mathbf{c})\bigr),
        \end{equation}
        %
        where $(\boldsymbol{\gamma}_i, \boldsymbol{\beta}_i)$ are the
        scale/shift pair for the $i$-th LayerNorm and $\boldsymbol{\alpha}_i$
        is the corresponding gating vector. All six outputs are initialized to
        zero (``Zero'' initialization), ensuring that at the start of training
        each block is an identity function. %

    \item \textbf{Pre-Norm Modulation.} The input sequence
        $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times L \times d}$ is normalized and
        modulated:
        %
        \begin{equation}\label{eq:prenorm}
            \hat{\mathbf{X}} = (1 + \boldsymbol{\gamma}_1) \odot \mathrm{LN}(\mathbf{X})
                              + \boldsymbol{\beta}_1.
        \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{Mamba-2 SSD Sequence Mixer.} The modulated tokens
        $\hat{\mathbf{X}}$ are cast to \texttt{bfloat16} (the native precision
        of the fused CUDA kernel), forced contiguous, and passed through the
        Mamba-2 module. The continuous-time state-space dynamics are:
        %
        \begin{equation}\label{eq:ssm-ct}
            \dot{\mathbf{h}}(t) = \mathbf{A}\,\mathbf{h}(t) + \mathbf{B}\,x(t),
            \quad
            y(t) = \mathbf{C}\,\mathbf{h}(t),
        \end{equation}
        %
        discretized via the Zero-Order Hold (ZOH) rule with a
        \emph{data-dependent} step size $\Delta_t$:
        %
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ssm-disc}
            \bar{\mathbf{A}}_t = \exp(\Delta_t \mathbf{A}),
            \quad
            \bar{\mathbf{B}}_t = (\Delta_t \mathbf{A})^{-1}
                (\bar{\mathbf{A}}_t - \mathbf{I})\,\Delta_t \mathbf{B}_t.
        \end{equation}
        %
        While this represents the generalized continuous-time discretization utilized in prior SSMs, Mamba-2's Structured State Space Duality (SSD) framework specifically restricts $\mathbf{A}$ to a scalar identity structure (effectively $\mathbf{A} = -\mathbf{I}$). This mathematically simplifies the state transition to $\bar{\mathbf{A}}_t = \exp(-\Delta_t)$, unlocking highly optimized hardware-level matrix multiplications. The selective scan applies this recurrence causally over the 1D token sequence, maintaining a hidden state $\mathbf{h}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{state}}}$ with $d_{\mathrm{state}} = 64$.

    \item \textbf{Gated Residual.} The mixer output is gated before the
        residual connection:
        %
        \begin{equation}\label{eq:gate1}
            \mathbf{X} \leftarrow \mathbf{X}
            + \boldsymbol{\alpha}_1 \odot \mathrm{Mamba2}(\hat{\mathbf{X}}).
        \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{MLP with Second AdaLN.} A second pre-norm modulation using
        $(\boldsymbol{\gamma}_2, \boldsymbol{\beta}_2)$ precedes a two-layer
        feedforward network with GELU activation:
        %
        \begin{equation}\label{eq:mlp}
            \mathrm{MLP}(\mathbf{z}) = \mathbf{W}_2\,\mathrm{GELU}(\mathbf{W}_1\,\mathbf{z} + \mathbf{b}_1) + \mathbf{b}_2,
        \end{equation}
        %
        with hidden dimension $4d = 1536$. The result is gated by
        $\boldsymbol{\alpha}_2$ before the final residual:
        %
        \begin{equation}\label{eq:gate2}
            \mathbf{X} \leftarrow \mathbf{X}
            + \boldsymbol{\alpha}_2 \odot \mathrm{MLP}\bigl(
                (1+\boldsymbol{\gamma}_2) \odot \mathrm{LN}(\mathbf{X})
                + \boldsymbol{\beta}_2
            \bigr).
        \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{The \texttt{ngroups} divisibility constraint.}
The \texttt{causal\_conv1d} kernel organizes the inner dimension into
\emph{groups} of size \texttt{headdim}, yielding
$\texttt{ngroups} = d_{\mathrm{inner}} / \texttt{headdim}$. For correct
memory stride alignment on the H100 Tensor Cores (which operate on 8-wide
\texttt{bfloat16} vectors), \texttt{ngroups} must be divisible by 8. Given our
fixed $d_{\mathrm{inner}} = 768$, the choice $\texttt{headdim} = 48$ yields
$\texttt{ngroups} = 16$, satisfying $16 \bmod 8 = 0$. Alternative choices
such as $\texttt{headdim} = 64$ (yielding $\texttt{ngroups} = 12$, which
violates $12 \bmod 8 \neq 0$) would trigger a runtime error in the fused
kernel. This constraint is validated programmatically at model construction
time. %

\paragraph{State dimension trade-off.}
The SSM state dimension $d_{\mathrm{state}} = 64$ balances expressivity
(capacity to carry identity and motion information across the temporal context)
against memory cost. Each layer maintains a per-batch recurrent state of size
$d_{\mathrm{inner}} \times d_{\mathrm{state}} = 768 \times 64 = 49{,}152$
parameters in \texttt{bfloat16}, or 96\,KiB per layer per batch element ---
negligible relative to HBM3 capacity. %

\subsection{Sweep-4 Cross-Scan for Spatial Token Ordering}\label{subsec:cross-scan}

Mamba-2 processes tokens in strict causal (left-to-right) order. For 2D
spatial content, a single row-major rasterization introduces directional bias:
tokens in the upper-left corner lack receptive field coverage of the
lower-right. We mitigate this via a \emph{Sweep-4 cross-scan}, applied
independently to each frame's spatial token block ($h \times w = 4 \times 4 =
16$ tokens): %

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Row-major forward (canonical rasterization)
    \item Row-major reverse (bottom-right $\rightarrow$ top-left)
    \item Column-major forward (transposed grid)
    \item Column-major reverse
\end{enumerate}

The four sweeps are batch-concatenated along the batch dimension to maximize
GPU kernel utilization (all four run in a single Mamba-2 \texttt{forward} call
at $4B$ effective batch size), then their outputs are summed after undoing each
scan permutation:
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cross-scan}
    \mathbf{Y}_{\mathrm{spatial}} = \sum_{s \in \{\mathrm{hw}, \mathrm{hw}^{-1}, \mathrm{wh}, \mathrm{wh}^{-1}\}}
        \pi_s^{-1}\bigl(\mathrm{Mamba2}(\pi_s(\mathbf{X}_{\mathrm{frame}}))\bigr),
\end{equation}
%
where $\pi_s$ denotes the permutation corresponding to scan direction $s$.
Non-spatial tokens (e.g., audio embeddings, if present in the main sequence)
remain on the canonical causal timeline and bypass the cross-scan. %

\section{Context Window and Dual-Stream Processing}\label{sec:context-window}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Token Construction Pipeline}

The input to the backbone is a single 1D sequence formed by concatenating
patchified embeddings from multiple modality streams. Each video frame (user
or avatar) is a latent of shape $C \times H \times W = 16 \times 16 \times 16$,
which is patchified by a learned \texttt{Conv2d} with kernel size and stride
equal to \texttt{patch\_size} $= 4$, producing:
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:patches}
    N_{\mathrm{patches}} = \left(\frac{H}{p}\right) \times \left(\frac{W}{p}\right)
    = \frac{16}{4} \times \frac{16}{4} = 16
    \quad \text{tokens per frame.}
\end{equation}

Each token is projected into the hidden dimension $d=384$ via the patch
embedding convolution. Three additive positional/type signals are applied: %

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Spatial position embedding}
        $\mathbf{E}_{\mathrm{pos}} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times 16 \times 384}$:
        learned, shared across all frames.
    \item \textbf{Temporal position embedding}
        $\mathbf{E}_{\mathrm{time}} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times (2T+1) \times 384}$:
        learned, indexed by frame position within the window (supporting up to
        $2 \times 7 + 1 = 15$ frame slots).
    \item \textbf{Stream type embedding}
        $\mathbf{E}_{\mathrm{stream}} \in \mathbb{R}^{5 \times 384}$:
        differentiates the five stream types (0=user visual, 1=avatar visual,
        2=noisy target, 3=avatar anchor, 4=user audio).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Dual-Stream Sequence Layout}

Given context length $T=7$, the full 1D sequence processed by the Mamba-2
backbone is ordered as follows (when \texttt{anchor\_position="last"}): %

\begin{equation}\label{eq:seq-layout}
    \mathbf{x} = \bigl[\,
        \underbrace{\mathbf{U}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{U}_T}_{T \times 16 = 112\text{ tokens}}
        \;\|\;
        \underbrace{\mathbf{V}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{V}_T}_{T \times 16 = 112\text{ tokens}}
        \;\|\;
        \underbrace{\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{A}, \mathbf{A}, \mathbf{A}}_{4 \times 16 = 64\text{ tokens}}
        \;\|\;
        \underbrace{\mathbf{Z}}_{ 16\text{ tokens}}
    \,\bigr],
\end{equation}
%
where $\mathbf{U}_i$ denotes the $i$-th user frame's 16 patch tokens,
$\mathbf{V}_i$ the avatar history, $\mathbf{A}$ the anchor (repeated $4\times$,
see Section~\ref{sec:anchor-last}), and $\mathbf{Z}$ the noisy target. The
total sequence length is therefore:
%
\begin{equation}
    L = 2T \times N_{\mathrm{patches}} + 4 \times N_{\mathrm{patches}} + N_{\mathrm{patches}}
      = (2 \times 7 + 4 + 1) \times 16 = 19 \times 16 = 304.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{User stream attenuation.}
To mitigate source identity leakage, the user stream tokens are scaled by a
factor $s_u = 0.9$ after embedding. This soft attenuation reduces the
magnitude of user-correlated features relative to the avatar stream
($s_v = 1.0$), biasing the backbone's representational capacity toward the
target identity. %

\section{The Anchor-Last Paradigm}\label{sec:anchor-last}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Motivation: Causal Proximity to the Denoising Target}

In a causal (left-to-right) sequence model, information from earlier tokens
can only reach later tokens via the recurrent state. The capacity of the
Mamba-2 state ($d_{\mathrm{inner}} \times d_{\mathrm{state}} = 768 \times 64$
values) is finite; as the sequence grows, earlier tokens are progressively
compressed. We exploit this property by placing the identity anchor
\emph{immediately before} the noisy target in the 1D sequence, minimizing the
number of intervening tokens and thereby maximizing the anchor's influence on
the denoised output. %

\subsection{The LayerNorm Cancellation Problem and \texorpdfstring{4$\times$}{4x} Repetition}

A naive strategy to boost anchor saliency is to scale its token magnitudes by
a constant $\sigma$ (we use $\sigma = 2.5$, termed \texttt{anchor\_saliency\_scale}).
However, the LayerNorm operations within each \texttt{DiTMambaBlock}
renormalize the token representations at every layer, canceling the effect of
magnitude scaling after the first block. Formally, for a sequence of tokens
$[\mathbf{x}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_L]$, LayerNorm computes:
%
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{LN}(\mathbf{x}_i) = \frac{\mathbf{x}_i - \mu}{\sigma_{\mathrm{stat}}} \cdot \gamma,
\end{equation}
%
where $\mu$ and $\sigma_{\mathrm{stat}}$ are the per-token mean and standard
deviation. Scaling $\mathbf{x}_i \leftarrow \sigma \cdot \mathbf{x}_i$ merely
rescales $\sigma_{\mathrm{stat}}$ without affecting the normalized output. %

To circumvent this, we apply \emph{token-count repetition}: the 16 anchor
tokens are repeated $4\times$ along the sequence dimension, yielding $4 \times
16 = 64$ anchor tokens. This quadrupling has two complementary effects: %

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Increased state occupancy.} In the Mamba-2 selective scan,
        each anchor token writes into the SSM state via the $\bar{\mathbf{B}}$
        matrix. Four copies apply four consecutive updates, effectively
        saturating the recurrent state with anchor-correlated information before
        the target tokens are processed.
    \item \textbf{Attention mass invariance.} Unlike magnitude scaling, token
        repetition increases the \emph{number} of informative context entries
        rather than their norm, and is therefore invariant to any normalization
        layer.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Zero-Noise Anchor Conditioning}

The anchor frame is a single, fixed avatar identity reference
$\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times C \times H \times W}$ drawn from the
avatar's canonical appearance. It is \emph{always} presented as a clean
(noise-free) latent at diffusion timestep~0, regardless of the noise level
applied to the target frame. This ensures the anchor provides a stable
identity signal uncorrupted by the diffusion noise schedule. %

\subsection{Anchor Dropout for Classifier-Free Guidance}

During training, the anchor (along with user and avatar visual streams) is
zeroed with probability $p_{\mathrm{cfg}} = 0.10$ to enable Classifier-Free
Guidance (CFG) at inference~\cite{ho2021classifierfree}. This forces the model to learn an unconditional
generation mode that can be interpolated with the conditioned mode at sampling
time via the guidance scale $w$:
%
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\boldsymbol{\epsilon}}_\theta = (1 + w)\,\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t \mid \mathbf{c})
        - w\,\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t \mid \varnothing),
\end{equation}
%
where $\mathbf{c}$ denotes the full context and $\varnothing$ denotes the
zeroed (unconditional) context. We use $w = 2.5$ at inference. %

\section{State-Space Decoupling: Mitigating Conditioning Collapse}\label{sec:decoupling}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{The Conditioning Collapse Failure Mode}

When training the full model end-to-end (all 12 Mamba-2 layers jointly
optimized with AdaLN and MLPs), we observe a failure mode we term
\emph{conditioning collapse}: the SSM layers learn to ignore the timestep
conditioning $\mathbf{c}$ and produce a nearly constant output regardless of
the noise level $t$. This manifests as: %

\begin{itemize}
    \item Flat loss curves after the initial descent
    \item Generated outputs that resemble blurred averages of the training
        distribution
    \item Near-zero magnitude in the gating vectors $\boldsymbol{\alpha}_1$,
        indicating the Mamba output is being suppressed
\end{itemize}

We hypothesize this occurs because the Mamba-2 scan dynamics (which are
optimized for sequential pattern extraction) and the AdaLN conditioning (which
provides global timestep/noise-level context) compete for representational
capacity in the shared hidden dimension. %

\subsection{The \texorpdfstring{\texttt{torch.no\_grad()}}{torch.no-grad()} Decoupling Strategy}

Our solution partitions each \texttt{DiTMambaBlock} into two zones: %

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Frozen zone} (\texttt{torch.no\_grad()}): The Mamba-2 SSD
        sequence mixer. No gradients flow through the SSM parameters
        ($\mathbf{A}$, $\mathbf{B}$, $\mathbf{C}$, $\Delta$, or the short
        convolution weights).
    \item \textbf{Trainable zone}: The AdaLN modulation layers (both pre-norm
        stacks), the gating parameters ($\boldsymbol{\alpha}_1$,
        $\boldsymbol{\alpha}_2$), and the MLP.
\end{enumerate}

Formally, the forward pass of each frozen block computes:
%
\begin{align}
    \hat{\mathbf{X}} &= \mathrm{modulate}\bigl(\mathrm{LN}(\mathbf{X}),\, \boldsymbol{\beta}_1,\, \boldsymbol{\gamma}_1\bigr) \label{eq:frozen-prenorm} \\
    \mathbf{M} &= \mathrm{sg}\bigl[\mathrm{Mamba2}(\hat{\mathbf{X}})\bigr] \label{eq:frozen-mamba} \\
    \mathbf{X} &\leftarrow \mathbf{X} + \boldsymbol{\alpha}_1 \odot \mathbf{M} \label{eq:frozen-gate}
\end{align}
%
where $\mathrm{sg}[\cdot]$ denotes the stop-gradient operator. The AdaLN
modulation~\eqref{eq:frozen-prenorm} \emph{does} receive gradients (it learns
\emph{how} to condition the pre-normalized input), but the Mamba-2 computation
itself~\eqref{eq:frozen-mamba} is treated as a fixed feature extractor. %

\subsection{Rationale and Effect}

This regime leverages pre-trained Mamba-2 weights (from a prior geometry-only
phase) as a \emph{frozen temporal feature extractor}, while the AdaLN layers
learn to inject timestep-appropriate conditioning into the representation
\emph{before} and \emph{after} the scan. Crucially, this decoupling sets the architectural foundation for subsequent knowledge transfer from a pretrained diffusion transformer, ensuring the recurrent states can be mapped to stable global attention features without destroying their inductive biases. The MLP retains full trainability,
providing the model with sufficient capacity to adapt the frozen scan features
to the denoising objective. %

In practice, this strategy:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Eliminates conditioning collapse within 500 steps of resuming training
    \item Reduces per-step memory by $\sim$18\% (frozen SSM activations need
        not be stored for backpropagation)
    \item Permits larger batch sizes (8 vs. 6 on a single H100 80\,GB)
\end{itemize}

\section{Computational Feasibility}\label{sec:feasibility}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Hardware Configuration}

All experiments are conducted on the Narval and Mist HPC clusters, utilizing
NVIDIA H100 80\,GB HBM3 GPUs (SXM5 form factor, 700\,W TDP). Single-GPU
training is used throughout (no model or data parallelism), simplifying
reproducibility and eliminating communication overhead. Optimization is performed 
using the AdamW optimizer~\cite{loshchilov2019decoupled} with a base learning rate of $1.5 \times 10^{-4}$. 
To maximize Tensor Core throughput on the H100, the PyTorch Lightning trainer 
is configured for \texttt{bf16-mixed} precision, complementing the explicit 
\texttt{torch.bfloat16} casting within the Mamba-2 kernels. %

\subsection{Complexity Comparison}

For sequence length $L = 304$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Self-Attention (DiT baseline):} $\mathcal{O}(L^2 d) = \mathcal{O}(304^2 \times 384) \approx 35.5\,\text{M FLOPs/layer}$
    \item \textbf{Mamba-2 SSD:} $\mathcal{O}(L \cdot d_{\mathrm{inner}} \cdot d_{\mathrm{state}}) = \mathcal{O}(304 \times 768 \times 64) \approx 14.9\,\text{M FLOPs/layer}$
\end{itemize}

For a single causal scan, the Mamba-2 mixer provides a $2.4\times$ theoretical FLOP reduction per layer. The Sweep-4 spatial wrapper performs four directional scans per spatial frame, so the raw FLOP count of the spatial mixer is higher than the single-scan estimate. In practice, however, the four sweeps are batch-concatenated and executed by fused kernels, avoiding materialization of a full $L \times L$ attention matrix in HBM. We therefore treat the FLOP comparison as a single-scan reference point and report measured throughput separately in Table~\ref{tab:throughput}. The memory advantage of the recurrent state grows as context windows or latent resolution increase. %

\subsection{Inference: Recurrent State Handoff}

At inference time, the model operates in \emph{autoregressive} mode: each
generated frame becomes part of the context for the next. The Mamba-2
architecture enables $\mathcal{O}(1)$ state handoff between frames via the
\texttt{InferenceParams} mechanism: after processing the full context window,
the per-layer recurrent state
$(\mathbf{h}_{\mathrm{conv}}, \mathbf{h}_{\mathrm{ssm}}) \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{inner}} \times d_{\mathrm{conv}}} \times \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{inner}} \times d_{\mathrm{state}}}$
is cached and passed as the initial state for the next frame's denoising
diffusion loop. This avoids reprocessing the entire context history at each
frame, yielding constant-time per-frame generation regardless of the temporal
horizon. %

\section{Residual Audio Injection Bypass}\label{sec:audio-bypass}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

The visual architecture above is the main object evaluated in Chapters~\ref{ch:task} and~\ref{ch:visual-results}. Audio conditioning is treated as an extension point because the full avatar goal requires speech-driven motion, but naive insertion of dense audio tokens can damage the visual state. The bypass below was introduced as a design response to the audio failures discussed in Chapter~\ref{ch:audio}; it should be read as architectural preparation for future multimodal work rather than as a solved audio-conditioning result.

\subsection{Design Rationale}

A straightforward approach to audio conditioning would interleave audio tokens within the main 1D context sequence. This has two drawbacks:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Sequence length inflation.} With $T=7$ frames and $\texttt{tokens\_per\_frame} = 5$, audio would add $7 \times 5 = 35$ tokens to an already 304-token visual sequence.
    \item \textbf{Latent spatial smearing.} Audio is a 1D temporal signal. Placing it in the main causal sequence exposes it to visual token processing and can distribute speech information across non-mouth regions.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Architecture: Cross-Attention After Backbone}

Instead of embedding audio in the main sequence, we employ a \emph{Residual Audio Injection Bypass} positioned after the $D=12$ Mamba-2 layers and before the final linear projection:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:audio-bypass}
    \mathbf{Z}' = \mathbf{Z} + g \cdot \mathrm{CrossAttn}(
        Q{=}\mathbf{Z},\;
        K{=}\mathbf{E}_{\mathrm{audio}},\;
        V{=}\mathbf{E}_{\mathrm{audio}}
    ),
\end{equation}
%
where $\mathbf{Z} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 16 \times 384}$ are the target tokens extracted from the backbone output, $\mathbf{E}_{\mathrm{audio}} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times (T \cdot \texttt{tpf}) \times 384}$ are the embedded audio tokens, and $g$ is a learned scalar gate initialized to zero:
%
\begin{equation}
    g = \texttt{nn.Parameter(torch.zeros(1))}.
\end{equation}

Because this operation attends only from the 16 target spatial tokens to the audio sequence, it avoids a global $\mathcal{O}(L^2)$ computation over the entire visual context. Chapter~\ref{ch:audio} explains why this kind of separation is necessary and why the initial audio experiments remain future work rather than a completed contribution.

\subsection{Audio Embedding}

Audio is represented as quantized EnCodec tokens with $n_{\mathrm{cb}} = 32$ codebooks, vocabulary size 1024 plus a silence/padding index, and $\texttt{tpf} = 5$ tokens per video frame. Each codebook maintains an independent embedding table; the per-codebook vectors are summed before a temporal sub-frame positional encoding is added:
%
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{e}_{\mathrm{audio}}^{(t, k)} = \sum_{c=1}^{32}
        \mathrm{Embed}_c\bigl(\mathbf{a}^{(t, c, k)}\bigr)
        + \mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{sub}}^{(k)}.
\end{equation}
%
Here $t$ is the frame index, $c$ is the codebook index, $k \in \{1, \ldots, 5\}$ is the sub-frame position, and $\mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{sub}}^{(k)}$ is a learned sub-frame positional embedding.


\chapter{Visual Stabilization and Results}\label{ch:visual-results}

The previous chapters defined the avatar task, showed the baseline identity-leakage failure, and introduced the recurrent architecture. This chapter presents the interventions used to stabilize target identity and the results that reveal the boundary of the approach. The central finding is that identity and motion can be separated under clean teacher-forced context, but autoregressive self-conditioning remains the main source of long-horizon drift.

\section{Baseline: Unmitigated Mamba-2 DiT}

The unmitigated Mamba-2 DiT baseline is computationally viable, but it does not preserve target identity during autoregressive rollout. As reported in Chapter~\ref{ch:task}, the baseline reaches 93.00\% identity leakage and a mean swap score of $+0.342$ under autoregressive evaluation. Qualitatively, the avatar's generated geometry moves toward the source driver's facial structure, showing that the recurrent state has learned an unintended shortcut: use the driver not only for motion, but also for identity. Figure~\ref{fig:identity_bleed} illustrates this failure mode.

\section{Intervention 1: Anchor-Last Identity Grounding}

The first stabilization mechanism is the Anchor-Last context layout described in Section~\ref{sec:anchor-last}. The target avatar anchor is placed immediately before the noisy denoising target and repeated $4\times$ so that identity information occupies a larger portion of the recurrent state immediately before prediction. This design makes the target identity the most recent clean visual signal available to the causal scan.

\section{Intervention 2: Rank-1 Null-Space Projection}

Anchoring alone does not remove source identity from the driver stream. We therefore introduce Rank-1 Null-Space Projection to suppress the source user's static geometry before it enters the recurrent state. For the primary driver, we extract 952 source frames from the processed Seamless subset and flatten each latent into a 4096-dimensional vector. Singular Value Decomposition isolates the leading direction $v$ associated with the driver's dominant static appearance. We use a rank-1 projection as a minimal intervention: it removes the strongest source-identity direction while preserving as much dynamic motion variation as possible. During prefill, source-driver latents are projected with:
\[
    P = I - vv^T.
\]
This projection removes the dominant identity direction from the source stream, forcing the model to use source latents primarily for motion and the avatar anchor for identity.

\section{Intervention 3: Transformer-to-Mamba Feature Distillation}

The null-space projection addresses source identity bias, but the recurrent student still has weaker spatial inductive bias than an attention-based transformer. We therefore add Transformer-to-Mamba Feature Distillation (T2MD), using a frozen DiT teacher to guide the hidden representations of the Mamba-2 student. During Phase 4 training, we hook layer-level \texttt{DiTMambaBlock} outputs and apply an MSE loss between the student's spatial representations and the teacher's attention-based features.

\chapter{Audio-Visual Alignment and Future Work}\label{ch:audio}

The full digital-avatar goal requires speech-driven motion as well as visual motion transfer. The visual results in Chapter~\ref{ch:visual-results} show that identity can be stabilized under teacher-forced context, but they also show that autoregressive visual drift remains unresolved. Audio adds a second source of instability: dense speech representations must affect the mouth region without corrupting the target identity stored in the visual state. This chapter documents the audio integration attempt, the failures it exposed, and the architectural changes required for future work.

\section{Why Audio Matters}

Motion-driven avatars can follow head pose and expression from a source video, but realistic face-to-face interaction also requires speech synchronization~\cite{prajwal2020wav2lip}. Audio can provide timing and phonetic information that may not be recoverable from visual context alone. A speech-driven avatar therefore needs to route acoustic information into the lower face while protecting the upper-face geometry and identity features that should remain stable.

\section{EnCodec Integration Attempt}

We experimented with a high-bandwidth EnCodec representation~\cite{defossez2023high} operating at 24 kbps with 32 discrete codebooks. During the audio phase, the DiT routing modules were expanded with 32 parallel \texttt{nn.Embedding} tables that projected the discrete audio tokens into the same hidden dimension as the visual tokens. This design was attractive because it preserved detailed acoustic information, but it also introduced a high-variance stream that was much denser than the visual context.

\section{Failure 1: High-Variance Memory Flushing}

When the 32-codebook audio embeddings were introduced into the prefill context, the model lost coherent facial geometry. The likely cause is finite-state interference. The Mamba-2 SSD hidden state has limited capacity, and the unmasked audio embeddings presented a high-entropy signal that competed with the target identity anchor. Instead of adding localized speech information, the audio stream displaced useful visual identity information from the recurrent state.

This failure reintroduced the same source-attractor behavior studied earlier in the thesis. Once target geometry was weakened, the model again became vulnerable to source identity bleed under autoregressive uncertainty. To isolate the visual experiments in Chapter~\ref{ch:visual-results}, the audio pathway was muted with a tensor-level silence mask, $p_{audio}=1.0$, while preserving sequence shape.

\section{Failure 2: Latent Spatial Smearing}

The second failure mode was \textit{latent spatial smearing}. Speech should primarily affect the mouth and jaw. Global audio conditioning, however, can distribute acoustic information across the entire $H \times W$ face latent. When audio modulates upper-face regions such as eyes, forehead, hair, and skull shape, it destabilizes exactly the identity features that the visual system is trying to preserve.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \maybeincludegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{audio_smearing.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Latent Spatial Smearing vs. Spatial Bounding Box Masking.} Contrasting ungated global audio conditioning against a proposed bounded mask restricted to lower-face geometry.}
    \label{fig:audio_smearing}
\end{figure}

\section{Failure 3: Frame-Rate Desynchronization}

Temporal alignment across modalities introduced a separate data-pipeline problem. Visual frames were sampled at 15 fps, while EnCodec tokens were extracted from a 60 fps source video. A naive 1:1 indexing scheme therefore created a 1-in-4 phase error: the audio stream advanced four times faster than the visual stream. Over long windows, this frame-rate mismatch makes lip-sync supervision unreliable because the model is trained on misaligned speech and mouth motion.

\section{Proposed Fixes}

The audio failures suggest that future speech-conditioned recurrent avatars need explicit locality and alignment constraints. Two interventions follow directly from the observed failures:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Spatial Bounding Box Masking.} Audio cross-attention should be restricted to lower-face patch indices corresponding to the mouth and jaw. This protects upper-face identity features from acoustic variance.
    \item \textbf{Stride-Aware Indexing.} The dataset pipeline should enforce a strict $4i$ mapping between 15 fps visual frames and 60 fps audio tokens, keeping each visual frame phase-locked to the correct audio block.
    \item \textbf{Staged or low-rank audio adapters.} Audio should be introduced after visual identity stabilization, ideally through low-capacity adapters or zero-gated residual paths so the speech stream cannot immediately dominate recurrent memory.
\end{itemize}

\section{Boundary of This Thesis}

The audio experiments should be read as a documented negative result. This thesis demonstrates progress on visual identity stabilization and identifies where that stabilization breaks under autoregressive and multimodal pressure. It does not claim to solve high-fidelity speech-driven avatar generation. Instead, the audio results define the next research problem: how to inject dense temporal speech information into a recurrent visual generator without displacing identity memory, smearing across the face, or misaligning audio and video time.

\chapter{Conclusion}

This thesis studied continuous digital avatar generation as an identity-stability problem. A persistent avatar must preserve the target person's appearance while following a source user's motion over long autoregressive horizons. The work shows that linear-time recurrent sequence mixing is a useful architectural enabler, but the central challenge is not scaling alone. The central challenge is preventing the recurrent visual state from confusing who the avatar is with how the driver moves.

\section{Summary of Contributions}

The thesis first formalized continuous avatar generation as a dual-stream task in which target identity and source motion must be separated. It introduced latent-space diagnostics for source identity bleed and showed that an unmitigated recurrent baseline leaks toward the source user in 93.00\% of evaluated autoregressive frames.

The thesis then developed a Mamba-2 DiT hybrid architecture for this setting. Replacing quadratic self-attention with a linear Structured State Space Duality mixer makes long-context avatar generation computationally feasible, while the Anchor-Last layout, Sweep-4 cross-scan, and state-space decoupling address the visual biases introduced by a causal 1D scan.

Finally, the thesis introduced visual stabilization methods. Rank-1 Null-Space Projection suppresses the source driver's static identity direction, and Transformer-to-Mamba Feature Distillation transfers spatial structure from a frozen DiT teacher to the recurrent student. Under teacher-forced evaluation, these methods reduce observed identity leakage to 0.00\% on the evaluated cross-identity pairs. Under autoregressive rollout, however, leakage can reappear, showing that temporal self-conditioning remains unresolved.

\section{Final Remarks}
The results establish a specific, useful boundary. The model can separate identity from motion when the context is clean, but long-horizon generation still suffers when generated frames become their own future conditioning signal. This distinction between teacher-forced success and autoregressive drift is the main empirical lesson of the thesis.

The audio experiments identify a second boundary. Dense 32-codebook EnCodec conditioning can flush visual memory, smear speech information across the face, and desynchronize under naive audio-video indexing. Future speech-driven avatars will need localized audio routing, stride-aware data pipelines, and staged conditioning mechanisms that protect identity memory.

Overall, this thesis supports a measured claim: hybrid state-space diffusion architectures are promising for persistent digital avatars, provided their recurrent memory is explicitly constrained against identity collapse. Linear scaling makes the system possible; identity stabilization makes long-horizon generation usable.
